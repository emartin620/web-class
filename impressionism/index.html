Impressionism

1859-
 1926

The Impressionist era took place during the mid-to-late nineteenth century in France. The artists were united by their desire to break off from strictly academic artforms. They held an annual salon every year called the “Salon des Refusés,” translating to exhibition of rejects. Impressionist art was critiqued because of its use of intense colors and small brush strokes. Many people at the time found the style to be offensive and controversial. Impressionism is largely considered to be the first modern art movement because of its connection to modernization. With the invention of the railroad, artists would travel outside of Paris to paint more remote scenes. The name Impressionsim comes from Monet's painting called "Impression, Sunrise" and a critique of it saying that the artists were only painting "impressions."

Stylistically, impressionist painters separated themselves from traditional artists by removing themselves from the studio. They painted wherever inspiration struck out in the world, “en plein air.” Other impressionist artists found inspiration indoors in places such as cafes, ballet studios, and other scenes of modern life. Short paint strokes applied in opaque layers made a blurry effect. They didn't seek the perfect, real image that previous art styles did. Brighter colors made by synthetic pigments were shocking and gave paintings dimensions of light. Impressionism is known for the expression of moving light in its landscapes. Portraits were more unposed than more traditional portrait scenes. They conveyed reality through motion.

Artists

  • Mary Cassatt

    May 22, 1844 - June 14, 1926
    American
    Though born in Pennsylvania, she moved to France to pursue art. She was known for painting women's personal lives.

  • William Merritt Chase

    November 1, 1849 – October 25, 1916
    American
    He was a teacher who founded the Chase School which eventually became the Parsons School of Design. He is best known for his portraits.

  • Edgar Degas

    19 July 1834 – 27 September 1917
    French
    He is known as one of the founders of Impressionism and one of the best known artist of his lifetime. He was best known for his work depicting dancers.

  • Édouard Manet

    23 January 1832 – 30 April 1883
    French
    He is remembered for his piece “Olympia” which was pivotal in the transition to modern art. He is known for depicting modern, urban life in his works.

  • Berthe Morisot

    January 14, 1841 – March 2, 1895
    French
    Though she struggled to be taken seriously as an artist, her works were selected for six Impressionist salons. Morisot was known for painting women and children in domestic scenes.

  • Claude Monet

    14 November 1840 – 5 December 1926
    French
    He is known as the father of impressionist painting and the technique of painting landscapes outdoors. He was known for his use of color and light in landscapes.

  • Camille Pissarro

    10 July 1830 – 13 November 1903
    Danish-French
    As one of the older members of the group, Pissarro is remembered as a leader in the Impressionist movement. He was known for portrayals of the common man in natural settings.

  • Pierre-Auguste Renoir

    25 February 1841 – 3 December 1919
    French
    Before going to art school, he took singing lessons and worked in a porcelain factory. He is known for his portraits of women using bright colors.

  • Alfred Sisley

    30 October 1839 – 29 January 1899
    British-French
    He was inspired by Manet and Pissarro's styles. He was known for focusing landscape paintings more than any other Impressionist.
